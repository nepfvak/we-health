xīn — In Chinese medicine the Heart is the Emperor: sovereign of all organs, house of consciousness, and keeper of the spirit. Supporting it is not incidental — it is foundational.

概Overview

Needles, points, and whole-person care.

Functional cardiac concerns — palpitations, irregular rhythm, chest tension, and breathlessness that arise without identifiable structural disease — are among the most common presentations in Chinese medicine practice. From a TCM perspective, these symptoms often reflect a disturbance in the Heart organ system: either Heart Qi deficiency, in which the organ lacks the force to maintain steady rhythm and circulation, or Heart Blood deficiency, in which inadequate nourishment leaves the Shen (spirit) unanchored, producing anxiety, restlessness, and a fluttering pulse. Treatment is not aimed at the symptom alone but at restoring the underlying balance from which steady cardiac function naturally follows.

Acupuncture's influence on cardiovascular function is one of the better-researched areas of TCM science. Clinical studies have documented effects on heart rate variability (HRV), a key marker of autonomic nervous system health and cardiac resilience. By stimulating specific points along the Heart and Pericardium meridians, acupuncture appears to shift the autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance — the rest-and-regulate state — reducing the chronic sympathetic overactivation that strains the heart over time. Patients often report a pronounced calming of the chest following treatment, alongside improved sleep and reduced anxiety.

Dr. Zhao brings an exceptionally rare combination of credentials to cardiac care. He completed a seven-year clinical medicine program at Harbin Medical University and served as Attending Physician in both Internal Medicine and Cardiology before dedicating his practice to integrative Chinese medicine. He currently serves as Executive Director of the World Federation of Chinese Medicine Societies Cardiovascular Disease Committee — one of the world's leading bodies coordinating research and clinical standards at the intersection of Chinese medicine and heart health. Patients with cardiac concerns benefit from that dual lens: the diagnostic precision of biomedical cardiology alongside the constitutional depth of classical TCM.

史History

The Emperor organ in two thousand years of medicine.

In the Huangdi Neijing — the foundational text of Chinese medicine compiled during the Han dynasty — the Heart holds a position unlike any other organ: it is described as the "sovereign official," the lord from whom all vitality and clarity descend. Chapter 8 of the Suwen presents the famous allegory of the twelve officials: each organ is assigned a role in governance, but the Heart alone is emperor, and its radiance is the source from which the spirits of all the other officials shine. This was not merely metaphor. The Neijing understood the Heart as the seat of shen — consciousness, perception, and emotional life — making cardiac health inseparable from mental and spiritual wellbeing. Joy in excess scatters the Heart Qi; shock injures it abruptly; prolonged grief exhausts the Lung but eventually depletes the Heart Blood that anchors the spirit. Emotional medicine and cardiac medicine were, from the beginning, the same discipline.

Dr. Zhao's training spans both traditions with unusual depth. He completed a seven-year program in clinical medicine at Harbin Medical University — one of China's leading institutions — where his clinical rotations included cardiology and internal medicine. That biomedical foundation was later integrated with advanced TCM training, producing a practitioner who can interpret an EKG and a pulse diagnosis within the same consultation. His role as Executive Director of the World Federation of Chinese Medicine Societies Cardiovascular Disease Committee reflects a career spent not simply practicing at this intersection, but helping define the international standards by which it is studied, taught, and applied.

At the wrist crease, on the little-finger side, in the hollow at the base of the hand.

On the inner wrist, two finger-widths above the wrist crease between the two tendons.

Cardiac use: Regulates Heart rhythm and eases chest constriction. One of the most studied acupuncture points for cardiovascular symptoms.

In the center of the palm, between the 2nd and 3rd metacarpals.

Cardiac use: Clears Heart fire and calms agitation. Used for palpitations with a racing sensation and heat in the chest.

At the vertex of the head, on the midline.

Cardiac use: Regulates autonomic nervous system tone — supports parasympathetic (rest) response and reduces sympathetic overactivation that strains the heart.

At the inner elbow, in the hollow at the end of the crease when the arm is bent.

Cardiac use: Nourishes Heart Yin and calms the mind. Used when insomnia is accompanied by night sweats and palpitations.

Sanyinjiao
三陰交

Above the inner ankle, three finger-widths above the tip of the medial malleolus.

Cardiac use: Meeting point of the three Yin leg meridians. Nourishes Yin and Blood, supporting vascular tone and blood pressure regulation.
