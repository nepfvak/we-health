罐 Cupping

guàn — Glass cups draw stagnant blood and qi to the surface, freeing tight muscle and clearing channel obstruction.

概Overview

Drawing stagnation to the surface.

Cupping is a therapy that uses glass, silicone, or plastic cups placed on the skin to create suction. The goal is often to relieve muscle tension, improve local circulation, and reduce feelings of tightness or stiffness. It is frequently used on the back, shoulders, and legs.

Some patients feel relaxed or notice temporary pain relief after cupping. It may be especially appealing for sore muscles or a sense of "stagnation," but the research is still limited, and benefits can be short-term. The circular marks left behind are common and usually fade in a few days.

Cupping can cause bruising, tenderness, burns, skin irritation, or, rarely, infection. It should not be done over broken skin, varicose veins, or areas with poor sensation. Wet cupping carries greater risk and should only be done by properly trained professionals using strict sterile technique.

史History

Ancient suction, modern recovery.

Cupping (拔罐, báguàn) appears in Chinese medical literature as early as the Eastern Jin dynasty (317–420 CE), with early references in the writings of the alchemist-physician Ge Hong, who described using animal horn cups to draw out toxins and stagnant blood. The technique was refined through successive dynasties, with ceramic and later glass cups replacing animal horns as medicine moved away from folk practice into formal clinical doctrine.

The theoretical basis draws on the concept of blood stasis (瘀血, yūxuè) — a condition in which blood and Qi cease to circulate freely, pooling in the tissues to produce pain, tension, and dysfunction. The circular marks left by cupping are not bruises in the Western sense but a visible expression of stagnation being drawn to the surface. In classical theory, bringing stagnation to the skin layer — closer to the body's exterior — makes it accessible to the body's own clearing mechanisms. The darker the mark, the deeper the stagnation.

Cup Type

Fire Glass Cupping

A flame is briefly introduced inside a glass cup to exhaust the oxygen, then the cup is quickly placed on the skin. As the air cools, a vacuum forms, drawing the skin and underlying tissue upward.

Fire cupping is the most traditional and precise form. Glass transmits heat, providing an additional warming effect at the point of application. The practitioner controls intensity by adjusting the size of the cup and placement speed.

SuctionIntense and precise
SensationStrong pulling warmth
MarkingModerate to significant
Best forDeep chronic tension, cold patterns
Cup Type

Silicone Cupping

Soft silicone cups are squeezed to expel air, then placed on the skin. As the cup expands back to its natural shape, it creates suction. Pressure is easily adjusted by squeezing the cup during treatment.

Silicone cups are ideal for sensitive areas, patients new to cupping, or situations where precise suction control is needed. They are also preferred for sliding techniques because their smooth edges glide easily over oiled skin.

SuctionMild to moderate, adjustable
SensationGentle pulling pressure
MarkingMinimal to moderate
Best forSensitive skin, facial cupping, sliding technique
Cup Type

Bamboo Cupping

Bamboo cups are boiled in an herbal decoction or heated with steam before application. The heat creates suction as the bamboo cools on the skin, simultaneously delivering the therapeutic properties of the herbal preparation through the pores.

This is one of the oldest cupping methods, particularly prized in some lineages for its ability to combine topical herbal medicine with the mechanical effects of suction. The bamboo gives a softer, more diffuse pull than glass.

SuctionSoft and diffuse
SensationWarm, broad pressure
MarkingLight to moderate
Best forHerbal steam therapy, whole-back treatment
Technique

Stationary Cupping

Cups are applied and left in place for 5–15 minutes without moving. This allows sustained decompression of the tissue beneath each cup, encouraging blood flow to the area and releasing deep layers of fascial tension.

Stationary cupping produces the most significant circular marks. These are a normal response to the lifting of blood to the surface and indicate the degree of stagnation in the treated area — not damage to the tissue.

Duration5–15 minutes per placement
MarkingSignificant, fades 3–7 days
Best forChronic tension, deep myofascial release
Technique

Sliding Cupping

The skin is generously oiled, then cups with lighter suction are moved across the surface in long strokes. The movement lifts and moves the tissue in sequence, much like a deep-tissue massage but working from the inside out.

Sliding cupping covers a large surface area quickly and provides a more even distribution of stimulation. It produces less intense marking than stationary cupping and is particularly effective for broad back tension or post-workout recovery.

Duration5–20 minutes covering area
MarkingLight streaks, fade quickly
Best forBroad muscle tension, athletic recovery
Technique

Flash Cupping

Cups are applied and immediately removed in a rapid sequence across an area. Each application lasts only one to two seconds. The brief, repeated suction stimulates the skin and superficial circulation without the sustained pull of stationary cupping.

Flash cupping is used when a lighter, more dispersing effect is needed — for example, over the lung area to help clear a lingering cough, or when minimal marking is preferred. It can be applied quickly over a large area.

Duration1–2 seconds per application
MarkingMinimal to none
Best forRespiratory support, dispersing technique, sensitive patients
