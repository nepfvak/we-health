罐 Cupping

guàn — Glass cups draw stagnant blood and qi to the surface, freeing tight muscle and clearing channel obstruction.

概Overview

Drawing stagnation to the surface.

Cupping is one of those therapies that looks dramatic and feels, to most patients, profoundly relieving. Glass or silicone cups are applied to the skin — typically the back, shoulders, or thighs — and a vacuum is created inside each cup, either by briefly introducing a flame (fire cupping) or by a mechanical pump. The suction lifts the soft tissue upward, drawing blood to the surface of the skin and creating the characteristic reddish or purple marks that fade within three to seven days.

In Chinese medicine theory, these marks are not bruises — they are the expression of stagnant blood and qi being moved to the surface for dispersal. The darker the mark in a given area, the more congestion was present. Practitioners use the pattern of colouration as additional diagnostic information: a very dark mark over the lung region, for instance, may indicate accumulated cold in the respiratory system.

Beyond its classical role, cupping has become well-studied for its effects on myofascial release, local circulation, and pain reduction. Athletes have embraced it widely. For patients carrying chronic tension in the upper back and neck — often linked to stress, postural demands, or stagnant qì in the liver channel — a cupping session can produce relief that hours of massage could not reach.

What to expect during a session

  • You will lie face-down on the treatment table while the practitioner positions cups — typically 4–10 — along the back, shoulders, or other target areas. Sessions usually last 10–20 minutes.
  • The sensation is one of strong pressure and warmth, not sharp pain. Most patients find it deeply satisfying, especially over areas of longstanding tightness.
  • Circular marks ranging from pale pink to deep purple will appear where each cup was placed. These are temporary and typically clear within three to seven days.
  • Flash cupping — rapid application and removal without retention — is used when a lighter, more dispersing technique is appropriate and leaves minimal marking.

Commonly reported benefits

  • Rapid relief of upper back, neck, and shoulder tension that has not responded to other approaches
  • Loosening of myofascial restrictions and reduced muscle guarding
  • Support during respiratory illness — cupping on the upper back can help clear lingering cough and chest congestion
  • Reduction of headaches rooted in neck and shoulder stagnation
  • Improved local circulation and a visible lightness of skin tone in treated areas
  • Psychological relief — many patients describe a sense of having been thoroughly unwound
