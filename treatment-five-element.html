五行Five Element Acupuncture

五行 Five Element Acupuncture

wǔ xíng — A classical system of diagnosis and treatment that maps the body, mind, and spirit to the five movements: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water.

概Overview

Treating the person, not only the pattern.

Five Element Acupuncture is a classical style of Chinese medicine that treats the whole person — body, mind, and spirit together. Rather than focusing only on symptoms, it seeks the underlying cause of imbalance: one primary element that is most disturbed. Every patient's care begins with identifying this constitutional element through observation, listening, colour, odour, emotion, and sound.

Each of the five elements — Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water — corresponds to organ systems, seasons, emotions, colours, and stages of life. When an element falls out of balance, symptoms may appear on many levels at once: physical pain and fatigue, emotional difficulty, mental fog, and a sense of disconnection. Five Element treatment addresses all of these simultaneously.

One of its most distinctive qualities is its attention to the spirit — the shen (神). Practitioners trained in this tradition observe how a patient moves through the world, not only how their body feels today. This approach is particularly effective for chronic conditions, complex presentations where standard treatment has plateaued, and for those seeking a more integrative form of care.

史History

Five thousand years of elemental medicine.

Five Element theory (五行, wǔ xíng) is among the oldest and most philosophically complete frameworks in Chinese medicine. Its roots lie in Daoist cosmology, where all phenomena arise from and return to the interplay of five phases of transformation: Wood (木, growth), Fire (火, expansion), Earth (土, transformation), Metal (金, contraction), and Water (水, consolidation). These five movements describe not fixed substances but dynamic relationships — each generating the next, each restraining another in a continuous cycle.

The classical text most associated with this tradition is the Huangdi Neijing (黃帝內經), the Yellow Emperor's Classic of Medicine, compiled over centuries beginning around 200 BCE. It describes how the five elements correspond to organ pairs, seasons, emotions, sensory organs, colours, sounds, and tastes — giving practitioners a complete map from the outer world to the inner body. J.R. Worsley (1923–2003), a British acupuncturist, studied this classical tradition in the East and brought it to the West, establishing a distinctive school that preserves the original emphasis on the spirit as central to healing.

Wood — 木

mù · Spring · Growth

Wood is the element of new growth, vision, and forward movement. In the body it governs the Liver and Gallbladder — the organs responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When Wood is balanced, a person has clear direction, the ability to plan and follow through, and an even emotional tone. Flexibility and resilience are Wood qualities.

When Wood is disturbed, Qi becomes stuck or explosive. Physically this can manifest as tightness in the sides of the body, visual disturbances, migraines along the temples or top of the head, and tendon or ligament problems. Emotionally the person may feel frustrated, irritable, or unable to move forward. Menstrual irregularity and PMS commonly involve a Wood imbalance.

Fire — 火

huǒ · Summer · Expansion

Fire is the element of warmth, connection, and joy. It governs the Heart and Small Intestine, and in Five Element theory also the Pericardium and Triple Warmer — organs concerned with protecting the heart, regulating warmth, and governing relationships. A balanced Fire element brings an easy warmth toward others, clarity of mind, and genuine joy in life.

When Fire is disturbed, the heart becomes either over-stimulated or empty. Anxiety, palpitations, insomnia, and a troubled shen (spirit) are common signs. The person may laugh at inappropriate moments, crave constant connection, or feel an absence of joy even when circumstances are good. Tongue-related symptoms — speech difficulties, biting the tongue, ulcers — can also signal Fire imbalance.

Earth — 土

tǔ · Late Summer · Transformation

Earth is the element of nourishment, stability, and belonging. It governs the Spleen and Stomach — the organs of digestion and the transformation of food into Qi and Blood. Earth is associated with late summer, the pivot point between the active and retiring seasons. A balanced Earth element brings groundedness, the ability to receive and give nourishment, and a sense of being at home.

When Earth is imbalanced, the person may struggle to nourish themselves — physically or emotionally. Digestive difficulties, bloating, loose stools, fatigue after eating, and poor appetite are physical signs. Mentally the person may overthink, ruminate, or have difficulty finding firm ground. Worry is the emotion of Earth; when excessive, it disrupts the Spleen's ability to transform and transport.

Metal — 金

jīn · Autumn · Contraction

Metal is the element of structure, refinement, and letting go. It governs the Lung and Large Intestine — organs of intake and release, breath and elimination. Autumn is Metal's season: the time of harvest, of drawing inward, of releasing what is no longer needed. A balanced Metal element brings the ability to find and appreciate quality and value, to set clear boundaries, and to let go gracefully.

When Metal is disturbed, a person may hold on too tightly — to grief, to the past, to physical tension. Respiratory conditions such as asthma, recurring colds, and sinus congestion are common Metal presentations. The skin, as the outer boundary of the Lung, can also be affected. Emotionally, unresolved grief or an inability to find what is truly valuable in life signals Metal imbalance.

Water — 水

shuǐ · Winter · Consolidation

Water is the element of depth, will, and ancestral essence. It governs the Kidney and Bladder — the organs that store the body's fundamental vitality (Jing, 精) and regulate the deepest reserves of energy. Winter is Water's season: a time of stillness, conservation, and turning inward. A balanced Water element brings courage, inner strength, stamina, and the wisdom to know when to act and when to rest.

When Water is depleted or disturbed, the person may experience deep fatigue that no amount of sleep resolves, bone and joint degeneration, low back and knee weakness, reproductive difficulties, and hearing problems. Fear — the emotion of Water — may surface as chronic anxiety, phobias, or a pervasive unease. Water imbalance is common in those who have overworked for long periods, experienced significant trauma, or are moving through major life transitions.
