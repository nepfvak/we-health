草 Herbal Medicine

cǎo — Custom formulas of roots, barks, and flowers, decocted or granulated to the constitution — not the diagnosis.

概Overview

A pharmacy shaped to one person.

Herbal medicine uses plant-based formulas such as roots, bark, leaves, flowers, and seeds to support health and treat symptoms. In traditional Chinese medicine, herbs are often combined into individualized formulas based on the patient's overall pattern, rather than on a single diagnosis alone.

Patients may use herbal medicine for digestion, energy, sleep, stress, immune support, or chronic symptoms. Some formulas may help with specific complaints, but the effects depend on the formula, the condition being treated, and the quality of the herbs. Herbal medicine is often best used as part of a broader treatment plan.

Herbs can interact with prescription medications and may not be safe during pregnancy, breastfeeding, or for people with liver, kidney, or bleeding problems. Patients should always tell their clinician about every supplement or herb they are taking. Quality matters, so herbal products should come from trusted sources.

史History

Shennong's legacy and the living pharmacopoeia.

Chinese herbal medicine traces its mythological origin to Shennong (神農), the Divine Farmer — one of the Three Sovereigns of Chinese antiquity. Legend holds that Shennong systematically tasted hundreds of plants, minerals, and animals to discover their medicinal properties, suffering poisoning multiple times in the process. The text bearing his name, the Shennong Bencao Jing (神農本草經, Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica), compiled during the Han dynasty, catalogued 365 medicinal substances divided into three grades: superior medicines that nourish life, middle medicines that replenish vital energy, and inferior medicines that treat disease and must not be taken long-term.

Herbal formulas in Chinese medicine are rarely single-herb prescriptions. Classical texts describe complex multi-herb formulas structured like a court: a sovereign herb (君, jūn) addresses the primary pattern; minister herbs (臣, chén) support and enhance the sovereign; assistant herbs (佐, zuǒ) moderate the formula's strength or address secondary symptoms; and envoy herbs (使, shǐ) guide the formula to the correct organ system or harmonize the other ingredients. This conception of medicine as governance — the body as a kingdom requiring wise administration — is distinctly Chinese.

Root Herbs · 根
Foundation of vitality
Ginseng · Astragalus · Licorice root · Rehmannia

Root herbs form the backbone of restorative formulas. Dense with stored nutrients, they are classified primarily as tonics — substances that nourish the body's fundamental reserves of Qi, Blood, Yin, and Yang. Their depth makes them slow-acting but lasting in effect.

Bark & Wood · 皮
Warmth and movement
Cinnamon bark · Magnolia bark · Phellodendron · Eucommia

Bark and wood herbs tend to be pungent and aromatic, with a strong ability to penetrate the channels and dispel cold or dampness. Cinnamon bark (Ròu Guì) is among the most warming substances in the classical pharmacopoeia, used to reignite the body's yang fire.

花
Flowers & Buds · 花
Light, rising, clearing
Chrysanthemum · Honeysuckle · Magnolia flower · Safflower

Flower herbs are characteristically light and upward-moving — their nature reflects their form. They tend toward cooling, clearing heat from the upper body, head, and eyes, and dispersing wind or pathogens lodged in the exterior. Chrysanthemum is prized for brightening vision and calming the liver.

Seeds & Fruits · 子
Nourishing the deep
Schisandra · Jujube date · Wolfberry · Ziziphus seed

Seeds and fruits consolidate and nourish — they hold potential, like life stored in a kernel. They support the liver, kidneys, and heart, replenishing Jing (essence) and Blood. Schisandra (Wǔ Wèi Zǐ), which carries all five flavours, is particularly valued for stabilising scattered energy and supporting sleep.

葉
Leaves · 葉
Lung and surface clearing
Mulberry leaf · Loquat leaf · Perilla leaf · Artemisia

Leaf herbs are light and dispersing, suited to conditions of the surface, upper body, and respiratory system. Mulberry leaf (Sāng Yè) clears wind-heat and benefits the eyes; Loquat leaf (Pí Pá Yè) descends lung Qi and relieves cough. Their thin, expansive form reflects their clinical tendency to spread and open.

礦
Mineral · 礦
Anchoring the spirit
Oyster shell · Dragon bone · Magnetite · Cinnabar (historical)

Mineral substances carry exceptional weight — in both the literal and classical senses. Their heaviness anchors yang that has floated upward, settles an agitated shen (spirit), and calms the heart in patterns of palpitations, insomnia, or anxiety. Oyster shell (Mǔ Lì) and Dragon bone (Lóng Gǔ) are among the most commonly used in modern formulas.
