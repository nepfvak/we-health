孕 Fertility Support

yùn — Chinese medicine has supported reproductive health for two thousand years, treating not the isolated symptom but the whole terrain from which new life grows.

概Overview

Rooted in the body's fertile terrain.

Traditional Chinese Medicine views reproductive health through the lens of three foundational substances: Kidney Jing, Blood, and Liver Qi. Kidney Jing is the constitutional essence inherited at birth — the deep reservoir from which reproductive capacity is drawn. It governs the maturation of eggs, the quality of the uterine lining, and the hormonal rhythms that govern the menstrual cycle. Blood, in the TCM sense, is not merely the red fluid of physiology but the rich, nourishing medium that must be plentiful and freely moving to sustain a healthy cycle and support implantation. Liver Qi governs the smooth flow of emotion and physiological processes; when it stagnates — as it commonly does under chronic stress — the cycle becomes irregular, painful, or suppressed. Acupuncture, herbal medicine, and lifestyle guidance are deployed together to build these foundations rather than simply stimulate ovulation in isolation.

The conditions Dr. Selina addresses within the fertility support specialty are wide-ranging. Irregular or absent menstrual cycles, whether from stress, thyroid dysfunction, or constitutional deficiency, are among the most common presentations. Unexplained infertility — where conventional workup returns normal results yet conception does not occur — responds particularly well to TCM's whole-terrain approach, often revealing underlying patterns of Qi stagnation or Blood deficiency that Western diagnostics do not capture. IVF support is another major area: acupuncture in the weeks surrounding egg retrieval and embryo transfer has been shown to improve uterine blood flow, reduce procedural stress, and support implantation. PCOS and endometriosis-associated infertility, both complex hormonal and inflammatory conditions, are addressed through individualized protocols combining acupuncture with targeted herbal formulas.

Dr. Selina's specialization in reproductive health was formalized through her training under Professor Ming Jin, one of China's leading authorities in fertility acupuncture. Selected as one of twelve practitioners nationwide for the inaugural Overseas Chinese Medicine Mentorship — and the only overseas practitioner to receive this formal recognition — she brings a depth of clinical lineage that is rare outside of China. Treatment programs typically span three to six months, aligned with the natural cycles of ovarian recruitment. Patients are supported through every stage: cycle regulation, optimizing the fertile window, IVF preparation and recovery, and early pregnancy support where indicated.

史History

Rooted in the medicine of cultivation and life.

The classical foundations of reproductive medicine in Chinese thought are ancient and surprisingly precise. The Su Nu Jing — the Plain Girl Classic — is among the earliest texts to address reproductive physiology directly, describing the energetic conditions necessary for conception and the cultivation practices that support them. The Huangdi Neijing provided a more systematic framework, mapping reproductive function to two extraordinary vessels: the Chong Mai (衝脈), known as the "Sea of Blood," which governs the menstrual cycle and uterine nourishment; and the Ren Mai (任脈), the "Conception Vessel," which runs along the midline of the body and is understood to govern the uterus and the capacity to gestate. Classical physicians understood that the Kidney organ system — distinct from the anatomical kidney — was the root of all reproductive capacity, storing the Jing that determines fertility and vitality across a lifetime. Disruptions in these systems, whether from constitutional weakness, overwork, emotional suppression, or dietary excess, were the recognized causes of infertility two thousand years before endocrinology gave Western medicine its own vocabulary for the same phenomena.

Dr. Selina's clinical lineage in reproductive health is direct and formally recognized. She was selected as one of twelve practitioners nationwide for the inaugural Overseas Chinese Medicine Mentorship Program — and stands as the only overseas practitioner to receive this formal designation — under Professor Ming Jin, a specialist whose clinical work in reproductive acupuncture spans decades and whose protocols are taught across China's leading medical universities. This mentorship is not an honorary distinction but a rigorous clinical apprenticeship, transmitting not only technique but the reasoning frameworks and case-pattern recognition that distinguish master-level practice from standard training. The result is a fertility specialty that is simultaneously rooted in classical theory and informed by the most current evidence on reproductive acupuncture — a rare combination that patients traveling from across the mid-South region seek out at W&E Health Institute.
