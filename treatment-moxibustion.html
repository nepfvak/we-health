概Overview

Warmth as medicine.

Moxibustion is a traditional therapy that involves warming specific areas of the body with the heat from burning mugwort, an herb called moxa. It is often used alone or alongside acupuncture and is intended to warm the body, improve circulation, and support balance in traditional Chinese medicine.

Patients may seek moxibustion for cold sensations, joint stiffness, fatigue, or certain pain conditions. Some people find the warmth comforting and report temporary relief of discomfort or muscle tightness. It is generally presented as a supportive therapy rather than a stand-alone cure.

Because moxibustion uses heat and smoke, it can cause burns, skin irritation, or breathing discomfort in sensitive patients. It should be performed carefully by a trained practitioner, and extra caution is needed for people with asthma, sensitive skin, poor circulation, or reduced sensation.

What to expect during a session

  • Moxa (dried mugwort) is burned near or on specific acupuncture points to deliver gentle, penetrating warmth.
  • The sensation is typically warm and comforting — not painful; the practitioner monitors carefully to prevent overheating.
  • Sessions may be combined with acupuncture or used on their own depending on your condition.
  • The treatment room will have a distinct herbal smoke smell; smokeless moxa options are available.

Commonly reported benefits

  • Warmth and comfort for cold sensations or stiff joints
  • Support for fatigue or feelings of depletion
  • Temporary relief of muscle tightness or pain
  • Often used alongside acupuncture for a combined therapeutic effect
  • Grounding and calming for many patients

Moxa Stick

Most common · Indirect heat · Traditional

A cigar-shaped roll of compressed mugwort is lit and held 2–3 cm above acupoints, moved in gentle circles or bird-pecking motions. This is the most common form used in modern clinical settings.

The heat penetrates gradually and can be precisely directed. Practitioners rotate or move the stick to prevent scorching while allowing deep warmth to accumulate at the point.

SensationSpreading warmth, often described as deeply soothing
Best forCold limbs, joint stiffness, general tonification
SmokeModerate — herbal, distinctive smell
錐

Cone Moxa

Direct or indirect · Intense and focused · Classical

Small cones of mugwort are placed directly on the skin or on a medium such as ginger, salt, or garlic slices. The cone is burned down — often removed before it reaches the skin — delivering an intense, focused pulse of heat to a single point.

Cone moxa is considered the most classically potent form of moxibustion. Ginger-base cone moxa is especially popular for digestive and abdominal conditions; salt-base moxa on the navel is used for acute cold patterns.

SensationIntense, concentrated warmth at the point
Best forDigestive weakness, acute cold, specific point tonification
SmokeMore smoke than stick — good ventilation required
無

Smokeless Moxa

Modern adaptation · Low smoke · Sensitive patients

Smokeless moxa sticks are made from charcoal-processed mugwort or other compressed herbs and produce very little smoke while still generating therapeutic heat. Some practitioners also use moxa boxes with a fine-mesh filter.

This form is ideal for patients with respiratory sensitivity, asthma, or strong aversion to smoke. While some practitioners consider it slightly less potent than traditional moxa, it remains an effective option for warmth-based treatment.

SensationWarm and steady — slightly less penetrating than traditional
Best forRespiratory-sensitive patients, office or home use
SmokeMinimal — suitable for most environments
