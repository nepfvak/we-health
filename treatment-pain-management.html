痛 Pain Management

tòng — The body speaks through pain. TCM listens — tracing discomfort not to a single site but to the network of meridians, organs, and emotions beneath it.

概Overview

Needles, points, and whole-person care.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, pain is not an isolated symptom to be suppressed — it is a message. The classical understanding holds that pain arises wherever the free movement of Qi and Blood is obstructed. This principle, expressed in the foundational axiom "where there is no free flow, there is pain," frames every treatment decision. Rather than addressing the site of discomfort alone, TCM seeks the upstream cause: which meridians are blocked, which organs are depleted, and whether cold, damp, heat, or emotional tension is driving the pattern. Acupuncture needles placed along specific channels restore that flow, signaling the nervous system, releasing muscular holding patterns, and prompting the body's own analgesic and anti-inflammatory responses.

The range of pain conditions that respond well to acupuncture is broad. Chronic lower back pain is one of the most thoroughly researched applications, with multiple systematic reviews confirming its effectiveness beyond placebo. Neck pain, shoulder tension, and knee osteoarthritis have similarly strong evidence bases. Headaches and migraines — including tension-type and menstrual migraines — are among the most commonly treated conditions in Chinese medicine clinics worldwide. Peripheral neuropathy, carpal tunnel syndrome, and hip pain from sciatica or piriformis syndrome also respond favorably, particularly when treatment is sustained over a course of sessions rather than approached as a one-time intervention.

A course of treatment for pain typically involves weekly sessions over four to eight weeks, with the frequency tapering as the condition stabilizes. Dr. Selina conducts a thorough intake that maps not only the location and character of pain — whether dull, sharp, fixed, or moving — but also its relationship to rest, heat, cold, and emotional state. Needles are retained for twenty to thirty minutes; many patients report significant relaxation during treatment and reduced pain within the first few sessions. Realistic outcomes depend on the chronicity and complexity of the condition, but most patients with musculoskeletal pain experience meaningful improvement, and many achieve long-term remission with periodic maintenance care.

史History

From ancient battlefields to modern clinics.

The use of needling for pain is as old as Chinese medicine itself. Some historians trace the origins of acupuncture to wartime necessity — soldiers wounded by arrows occasionally reported that chronic ailments resolved after the injury, suggesting that puncturing certain sites of the body could release stagnation elsewhere. Whether apocryphal or not, this origin story captures something true about the classical theory: pain is obstruction, and relief comes through release. The Huang Di Nei Jing, compiled during the Han dynasty around 200 BCE, provided the first systematic account of pain through the concept of Bi syndrome — 痹證, literally "obstruction syndrome." Bi conditions arise when wind, cold, or damp invades the channels, causing pain, stiffness, and loss of mobility in the joints and limbs. The Nei Jing catalogues specific needling strategies for each variant, establishing a clinical framework that practitioners still reference today.

Modern evidence has validated what classical physicians observed empirically. The 1997 NIH Consensus Statement on Acupuncture formally recognized its effectiveness for postoperative pain, chemotherapy-induced nausea, and several pain conditions — marking a turning point in its acceptance within Western medicine. Subsequent decades brought acupuncture into hospital pain clinics, integrative oncology programs, and, notably, the United States Department of Veterans Affairs healthcare system. The VA's authorization of acupuncture as a covered treatment for chronic pain reflects both the scale of the need — musculoskeletal pain is the leading driver of disability claims among veterans — and the weight of accumulated evidence. Dr. Selina holds VA authorization to provide acupuncture treatment, bringing this classical lineage directly to bear on some of the most complex and treatment-resistant pain presentations.
