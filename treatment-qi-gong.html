气功 Qi Gong

qìgōng — Seated and standing breath-and-movement practice taught for daily self-cultivation between visits.

概Overview

The art of cultivating your own qi.

Qi gong is a traditional practice that combines posture, breathing, slow movement, and focused attention. It is designed to cultivate and regulate energy, but many people also experience it as a calming mind-body exercise. It can be practiced standing, sitting, or even lying down depending on the style.

Patients may use qi gong to reduce stress, improve breathing, support mobility, and promote a sense of mental clarity. Some studies suggest it may help with balance, fatigue, anxiety, and quality of life, although benefits vary. It is often used as a gentle daily practice rather than a one-time treatment.

Qi gong is usually low risk, but people with dizziness, balance problems, or pain should move carefully and avoid positions that feel uncomfortable. It should be adapted to the patient's ability level. As with any wellness practice, it works best when used consistently and alongside appropriate medical care.

史History

Cultivating life force through breath and stillness.

Qi gong (氣功) — 'the cultivation of Qi' — encompasses thousands of distinct exercises developed across millennia of Chinese religious, medical, and martial traditions. The oldest precursors appear in the Mawangdui silk texts (168 BCE), which include illustrated guides to daoyin (導引) — a system of stretching, breathing, and movement designed to guide Qi through the body and prevent illness. These exercises were practiced in Daoist monasteries and imperial courts alike, and their influence extends into virtually every branch of traditional Chinese medicine.

In Daoist cosmology, the human body is understood as a microcosm of the universe: the same Qi that animates the cosmos flows through the body's three cinnabar fields (三丹田, sān dāntián) — energy centers located in the lower abdomen, chest, and head. Qi gong practice aims to accumulate, refine, and circulate Qi through these centers, using breath as a vehicle and intention (意, yì) as its steering force. The legendary Daoist immortals (神仙) were said to have achieved extraordinary longevity through dedicated Qi cultivation — an idea that motivated millennia of practitioners to pursue what Chinese tradition calls 'nourishing life' (養生, yǎngshēng).
