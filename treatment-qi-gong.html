气功 Qi Gong

qìgōng — Seated and standing breath-and-movement practice taught for daily self-cultivation between visits.

概Overview

The art of cultivating your own qi.

Qi Gong — literally "qì work" or "the cultivation of vital breath" — encompasses a vast range of practices that share a common purpose: training the practitioner to sense, accumulate, and direct the body's own vital energy. Unlike Tai Chi, which has a martial arts origin and follows specific choreographed sequences, Qi Gong is more varied in form. It ranges from completely still meditation postures to slow, flowing movement sequences, breath-only practices, and visualisation exercises. What they share is deliberate attention to the breath and its relationship to the body's interior landscape.

In the context of Chinese medicine, Qi Gong is understood as self-cultivation between clinical treatments. Acupuncture and herbal medicine do their work during visits; Qi Gong is what the patient does for themselves the other six days of the week. It is not passive — it is an active participation in one's own healing. This is why classical practitioners considered a patient's willingness to practice at home a reliable indicator of their prognosis.

Medical Qi Gong — a formal branch of the tradition taught in Chinese hospitals alongside acupuncture and herbal medicine — uses specific practice protocols tailored to particular health conditions. Dr. Zhao's instruction at W&E Health Institute draws on both the classical self-cultivation lineages and the medical protocols, adapting each patient's practice to what their current pattern and lifestyle can sustain.

What to expect in instruction

  • Your first Qi Gong session begins with breath awareness — learning to feel the difference between habitual shallow breathing and the slow, abdominal breath that characterises all Qi Gong practice.
  • Initial sequences are short: one to three movements, practiced with a specific breath pattern and mental focus. Depth matters far more than duration at the beginning.
  • Seated forms are available for patients with limited mobility; standing forms are introduced when the patient is ready and able.
  • You will leave each session with a clear home practice — specific, achievable, and brief. Even five minutes daily produces meaningful change over time when the quality of attention is present.

Commonly reported benefits

  • Reduction of chronic stress and a lasting decrease in resting anxiety levels with consistent practice
  • Improved lung capacity and breath quality — particularly valuable for patients with respiratory conditions or post-COVID fatigue
  • Better sleep onset and quality, especially in patients who struggle to transition from the activity of the day to rest
  • Increased body awareness, including earlier detection of tension patterns before they become pain
  • A sense of agency over one's own health — the experience of doing something meaningful for oneself between clinic visits
  • Deepened benefit from acupuncture treatments when Qi Gong is practiced regularly alongside them
