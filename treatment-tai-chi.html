概Overview

Movement as a daily practice of health.

Tai chi is a gentle movement practice that combines slow, flowing motions with breathing and balance. It is often described as a moving meditation and is used to support physical and mental well-being. Many people practice tai chi for stress reduction, balance, flexibility, and overall health.

Research suggests tai chi may help improve balance, reduce fall risk in older adults, and support symptoms related to pain, arthritis, and anxiety in some people. Because movements are slow and low impact, it is usually accessible to beginners and people with limited fitness. It can be adapted for different ages and abilities.

Tai chi is generally safe, but patients with severe balance issues, joint pain, or medical conditions should start slowly and modify movements as needed. It should not cause sharp pain, and any movements that increase symptoms should be avoided. Regular practice tends to provide more benefit than occasional sessions.

What to expect in a session

  • Classes or sessions involve slow, deliberate sequences of movements paired with breathing.
  • No prior experience or fitness level is required — movements can be modified for anyone.
  • Progress is gradual; consistency matters more than intensity.
  • Comfortable, loose-fitting clothes are all you need — no special equipment.

Commonly reported benefits

  • Improved balance and coordination, especially for older adults
  • Reduced fall risk and greater confidence in movement
  • Support for stress, anxiety, and mental calmness
  • Gentle on the joints — suitable for people with arthritis or limited mobility
  • Can be practiced daily as an accessible wellness routine

The five principles of tai chi practice.

松 Principle 1 of 5
Sōng — Relaxation

The body remains soft and released at all times. Tension in any part disrupts the flow of movement and energy. In tai chi, relaxation is not passivity — it is an active, conscious releasing of unnecessary holding throughout the joints, muscles, and mind.

慢 Principle 2 of 5
Màn — Slowness

Slow movement builds awareness, balance, and internal sensitivity. Speed comes only after slowness is mastered. Moving slowly forces the practitioner to feel each transition — weight shifts, breath, and the quality of attention — rather than executing movements automatically.

圓 Principle 3 of 5
Yuán — Circularity

Every motion follows a curved, circular path. There are no sharp angles — movement flows like water. Circularity prevents force from blocking or stagnating at the joints, allowing energy to travel through the body in continuous, uninterrupted waves.

均 Principle 4 of 5
Jūn — Evenness

Pace, force, and attention remain steady throughout. No part of the form rushes or drags. Evenness trains the nervous system toward regulation — the body learns to resist the impulse to speed up when anxious or slow down when fatigued, finding a middle path instead.

靜 Principle 5 of 5
Jìng — Stillness

The mind remains calm and present. Tai chi is meditation in motion — stillness found within movement. As the body moves through the form, the practitioner cultivates a quality of inner quiet that, with practice, begins to carry into the rest of daily life.
