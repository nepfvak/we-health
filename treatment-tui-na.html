推拿 Tui Na

tuīná — Therapeutic manual work along the meridians — pressing, kneading, rolling — older than written medicine.

概Overview

Therapeutic bodywork guided by tradition.

Tui Na is a form of therapeutic Chinese massage that uses pressing, kneading, rolling, stretching, and other hands-on techniques. It is often used to address muscle tightness, joint stiffness, pain, and limited range of motion. Unlike relaxation massage, tui na is usually more targeted and based on traditional Chinese medicine principles.

Patients may benefit from improved mobility, reduced discomfort, and a feeling of release in tense areas. It is often combined with acupuncture, herbs, or other therapies. Some people also use tui na to support recovery from overuse or chronic tension.

Tui na can cause temporary soreness, especially if the treatment is deep or focused on tight areas. It may not be appropriate for people with fractures, acute injuries, blood clotting disorders, or certain skin conditions. Patients should speak up during treatment if pressure feels too strong.

史History

Hands as instruments of Qi and structure.

Tui na (推拿) — literally 'push and grasp' — is among the oldest manual therapies in Chinese medicine, with references to massage and physical manipulation in the Huangdi Neijing and in oracle bone inscriptions from the Shang dynasty (1600–1046 BCE). Early practitioners were called 'anmo' (按摩) physicians, a term that appears in court medical records of the Zhou dynasty, where massage specialists held official positions alongside acupuncturists and herbalists. The formal systematization of tui na as a distinct clinical discipline occurred during the Ming dynasty, when it was named and codified in its current form.

Tui na works along the same meridian pathways as acupuncture but uses the practitioner's hands — thumbs, palms, forearms, elbows — rather than needles. The goal is not simply to relax muscles but to regulate the flow of Qi and Blood through targeted pressure, rolling, and rotation techniques. Different hand methods correspond to different therapeutic aims: pushing (推) moves Qi along a meridian; grasping (拿) releases muscle tension and expels wind; kneading (揉) softens accumulations; and vibrating (振) disperses stagnation. The practitioner's own cultivation of internal Qi — developed through years of practice — is considered inseparable from clinical effectiveness.

Sustained finger or palm pressure on a specific point. Used to calm the nervous system and release local tension. The practitioner holds steady pressure — often for several breaths — allowing the tissue to soften and yield beneath the hand.

Gentle circular rubbing over the abdomen or affected area. Used to support digestion and ease surface tension. The motion is slow and rhythmic, with light contact — more soothing than penetrating, designed to move qi in the surface layers.

Rhythmic circular kneading of muscle tissue. Used for chronic stiffness and to improve circulation. The skin and underlying tissue move together in a rotating pattern — similar to kneading dough, but calibrated to the muscle's depth and tension.

A rolling motion using the back of the hand or forearm. Used to loosen large muscle groups in the back and legs. The knuckles and metacarpals roll continuously over the tissue, creating a deep wave-like penetration that reaches layers ordinary massage cannot access.
